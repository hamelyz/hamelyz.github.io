"I'm Not the Villain": Steve Hamel Pushes Back on Dolan League's Historic Punishment


The embattled GM of Tim Is Dead breaks his silence, defending the controversial moves and allegations that led to one of the harshest penalties in league history.

"I’m administratively vetoing this trade. Steve. No Chance[...] This not a laughing matter" Bryan "Silver"

Monday Evening in New York

I’m sitting in Steve Hamel’s office, a room that feels less like the headquarters of a fantasy sports franchise and more like the scene of a true-crime documentary. Corkboards line the walls, crowded with scraps of paper, half-erased diagrams, and pinned notes connected by tangled strings. It could be mistaken for brilliance—until you remember who made it.

Hamel wants you to know he’s not the bad guy, he’s just a misunderstood genius. Sure, his team Tim Is Dead is sitting at 1-3 and dead last in the West, and yes, he just orchestrated one of the most lopsided trades in league history, but this is all part of the grand plan—or so he insists.

“It’s not about where you are right now,” Hamel says, leaning back like he’s about to explain quantum physics. “We don’t use that word—tanking. We’re trying to build a championship team, and you have to take a step back to move forward.”

But co-commissioner Bryan "Silver" didn't buy it, slapping him with one of the harshest punishments in league history for “alleged tampering” and “undermining the integrity of competition.” Even as other GMs and fans pile on, Hamel refuses to back down, calling the criticism “overblown” and his moves “misunderstood.”

The Trade That Set the League on Fire

The punishment stems largely from the trade—the one that sent Giannis Antetokounmpo, a generational talent and perennial MVP candidate, to a rival team in exchange for rookie Alexander Sarr, a player whose best-case scenario is far from certain.

“Look, Giannis is incredible—no one’s denying that,” Hamel says, visibly annoyed. “But you have to think about the future. Alexander Sarr has the tools to become something really special. You don’t win championships by playing it safe.”

Critics—and the league—weren’t so charitable. The backlash was immediate, with league sources confirming that other team owners were “fuming” about the trade. One anonymous GM in the West put it bluntly: “I don’t know what [Hamel] is doing over there, but he’s making it impossible for the rest of us to take him seriously. You don’t just give away a player like Giannis—it’s a disservice to the entire league.”

When asked whether he regrets the deal, Hamel pauses for a moment before shaking his head. “It’s easy to criticize in hindsight,” he says. “But you have to make tough choices when you’re building something sustainable.”





Blame It on Tim

When asked why his tenure has been marked by disorganization, puzzling draft choices, and baffling free agency moves, Hamel shifts gears—to blaming the previous owner, Tim.

“Tim is very much alive." Rohit Raghunathan, longtime GM of the The Manhattan Men’s Hats

“When I came in, the cupboard was barren,” Hamel says, his tone heavy. “Tim left this team with no assets, no future—it was a mess.”

It’s a convenient scapegoat, but not everyone is buying it. “Hamel loves to talk about how Tim ruined everything,” a league source says, “but the team wasn’t that bad when he took over. Now? Now it’s bad.” As Rohit Raghunathan, longtime GM of the The Manhattan Men’s Hats, puts it: “Tim is very much alive.”

The disorganization has spilled over into game day, too. Lineups are shuffled moments before tipoff, key players ride the bench without explanation, and the team’s only win came after what one analyst called “the other team’s worst scoring week in a decade.”

Hamel brushes it off as growing pains. “Rome wasn’t built in a day,” he says.

“Sure,” an anonymous executive shot back, “but Rome didn’t bench its best architects either.”

The Bigger Picture (Supposedly)

For Hamel, every move—no matter how chaotic or indefensible—fits into a grand plan that only he seems to understand.

“If you want instant gratification, this isn’t the team for you,” he says. “We’re building something bigger than that. And when we get there, all the doubters will see.”

But for now, Tim Is Dead remains firmly at the bottom of the standings, and fans are left wondering if the promised future will ever arrive—or if it’s just another excuse to mask a team that can’t seem to get out of its own way.

Time will tell if Hamel’s “step back” will ever lead to a step forward. But if it doesn’t, one thing feels certain: Steve Hamel will find someone else to blame.
